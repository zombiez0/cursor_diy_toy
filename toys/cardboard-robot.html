üîß Step-by-Step Instructions

1

Prepare the Robot Body

Start with your large cardboard box. If it has flaps, fold them inside to create a solid rectangular body. This will be your robot's torso. Make sure the box is sturdy and can stand upright on its own.

Pro Tip: If your box is too big, you can cut it down to size. A cereal box works perfectly for this project!
2

Create the Robot Head

On the top of your box, draw a square about 4" x 4" for the robot's head area. Don't cut it out yet! Instead, draw the robot's face with two large circular eyes, a rectangular mouth, and any other features you'd like. Use markers to make it colorful and expressive.

Creative Idea: Try different expressions! Make your robot happy, surprised, or even grumpy. Each expression gives your robot personality!
3

Add Shiny Robot Details

Tear small pieces of aluminum foil and glue them around the robot's face and body to create a metallic, futuristic look. You can make buttons, control panels, or just decorative patterns. The foil will make your robot look like it's made of real metal!

Safety Note: Adult help may be needed when tearing foil. Make sure edges aren't sharp.
4

Create Robot Arms

Take your two cardboard tubes and cut them in half to make four arm segments. Use paper fasteners to connect two segments together for each arm - this creates an elbow joint that bends! Cover the arms with colored paper or foil to match your robot's design.

Engineering Tip: The paper fasteners act like real robot joints, allowing the arms to move and bend just like a real robot!
5

Attach Arms to Body

On each side of your robot's body, about 2 inches from the top, use paper fasteners to attach the arms. Push the fastener through the cardboard tube and through the box, then spread the fastener on the inside. Now your robot has moveable arms!

Adult Help: Making holes in cardboard might need grown-up help. Use a pencil to poke starter holes first.
6

Design Robot Legs

Cut two rectangles from cardboard, each about 6" tall and 3" wide. These will be your robot's legs. Decorate them with foil, colored paper, or markers to match your robot. Draw "robot feet" at the bottom with chunky, mechanical-looking details.

Design Tip: Make the feet slightly wider than the legs - this helps your robot balance better when standing.
7

Attach Legs for Walking Action

Here's the magic part! Attach the legs to the bottom of your robot's body using paper fasteners, but position them slightly forward (not directly underneath). This angle allows your robot to "walk" when you tilt it forward gently - the legs will move in a walking motion!

Physics Fun: The angled legs create a pendulum effect that makes the walking motion possible. It's real science in action!
8

Add Robot Antennae

Take your two bendy straws and poke them through the top of your robot's head. Bend them at fun angles - these are your robot's antennae! You can add small foil balls or colored paper flags to the ends to make them extra special.

Fun Fact: Real robots often have antennae for communication. Your robot is ready to receive signals from space!
9

Create Control Panel

On the front of your robot's chest, create an awesome control panel! Use colored paper circles for buttons, rectangles for screens, and foil strips for controls. Draw lines connecting different parts to make it look like a real robot's internal wiring.

Creative Challenge: What does each button do? Maybe one makes the robot dance, another makes it speak in robot language!
10

Final Assembly and Testing

Check all connections and make sure everything is securely attached. Test your robot's walking ability by gently tilting it forward on a smooth surface. The legs should swing and create a walking motion! Adjust the leg angle if needed for the best walking action.

Play Time: Your robot is now ready for adventures! Give it a name and create robot voices and stories. What planet is it from?
